// AI

A point of view, not a feature list.

In regulated, expert-heavy products, AI's job isn't to replace the expert. It's to remove the friction between the expert's judgment and the action. I build small tools to keep that opinion honest.

// THE TAKE

AI earns autonomy the same way a new hire does: narrow scope, a track record, then wider latitude. Anything else is a demo, not a system.

The teams that get this right embed AI inside the existing workflow (the system of record, the handoff, the queue) instead of bolting on a separate app the expert has to context-switch into. And they let trust compound in increments: tight scope first, human override always live, autonomy widening only as the system proves itself in that lane. Skip the increments and the failure mode shows up in production instead of in a demo, and in healthcare, the failure mode is the product.

I hold that view because I've built the workflow platforms where embedding, not add-on features, was the unlock. And Keystone, below, is that same trust-compounding logic applied to how I build with AI myself: narrow gate, human call, then the next stage opens.

PIC-TO-PLATE · INSIDE THE BUILD

Solo, concept to the App Store, in two months.

Product, design, and full-stack engineering, one person, one thesis: the friction in "what's for dinner" isn't ideas, it's not knowing what you already have.

The bet

Most recipe apps assume you already have a shopping list. Pic-to-Plate treats the pantry as a persistent model, not a one-off input, so it gets more useful with every session instead of starting over each time. Recipes surface across three effort tiers, Ready to Cook, Almost There, Go Shopping, instead of a single yes/no match, so a user who'd otherwise abandon at "you need 8 more ingredients" sees three things they can cook tonight.

Scope decisions for v1

No image storage Photos are processed and discarded, not kept, avoiding storage and privacy overhead before the model was validated.
No OAuth Email/password covers a soft launch; OAuth's redirect handling wasn't worth the surface area yet.
2 regenerations/day Keeps API cost predictable during validation without blocking the core value.
Desktop-first web Mobile responsive deferred so early web time went into the product experience, not breakpoints.
Feedback widget, soft-launch only Good signal during validation, marked for removal at public launch, no permanent debt.

One decision, in detail

Recipe generation takes 20-30 seconds. If nothing about your ingredients or preferences has changed since the last run, regenerating is wasted latency. Ingredients and preferences are hashed into a fingerprint; a matching fingerprint returns the cached result instantly. It's a small decision, but it's the difference between the app feeling instant on a second look and feeling slow every time.

Keystone AI

A human-in-the-loop orchestration layer for taking an idea to a validated MVP. Skills and agents do the ground work at each of six stages; the judgment, the constraints, and the go or no-go call stay with the expert in the loop, here, a product manager. Nothing advances without that call.

What it taught me: agents are confidently wrong often enough that the gates aren't overhead, they're the actual product-management contribution. Remove one to move faster and you ship something broken.

KEYSTONE AI · INSIDE THE BUILD

A director's process, not a prompt chain.

A repeatable operating system for taking an ambiguous idea to a validated MVP: agents do the execution labor, I make the go or no-go call at every stage.

Six stages, six gates

Signal intake Is this worth building at all?
Ideation & direction Which direction do we commit to, and what do I reject?
Spec synthesis Where's the MVP boundary, and what do I cut?
Build Which architecture tradeoffs do I accept or override?
Eval Ship or no-ship, given the risk?
Narrative Is this the true story, and what claim can I defend?

26 skills, one rule

Each stage draws on a library of versioned skill prompts, purpose-built for things like scoring ideas, stress-testing personas, cutting scope, framing tradeoffs, and catching overclaims. They all share one constraint: a skill can produce a verdict input, never the verdict itself. The call stays with me.

What's logged

Every gate produces a timestamped entry: what the agent proposed, what I overrode, and why. That log is the real artifact, more than any app it produces. It's judgment made auditable instead of asserted.

Where it stands, honestly

Stages run sequentially by hand today, a deliberate v1 boundary for a solo operator. Automating the handoff between stages is next. Eval coverage is only as strong as the test cases behind it, and writing good tests is still the harder half of the job, not something a gate can automate away.
